Long-Term Vision

He doesn't build to exit. He builds to endure.

Abubakar Hamisu Nasir Ahli — known across boardrooms and pitches as Sadeeq Ahli — is a young Nigerian entrepreneur whose work sits at the intersection of systems thinking and frontier ambition. As a youth building from Nigeria, his age is not a footnote — it is the point. Where others see fragmentation, he sees architecture. Where others chase markets, he builds infrastructure.

His approach is rooted in a rare discipline: the willingness to stay patient long enough to arrive with leverage. Through ventures across sports technology, oil and gas, manufacturing, and industrial supply chains, Sadeeq is laying the groundwork for a portfolio of companies that will shape how African economies operate — and how the world receives them.

The Architect of the Long Game: Sadeeq Ahli Is Building Africa's Next Industrial Empire — Quietly, Deliberately, and On His Own Terms

He is not chasing the next funding round. He is not optimizing for a headline exit. Abubakar Hamisu Nasir Ahli — known everywhere as Sadeeq Ahli — is doing something far more difficult and far more rare: he is building infrastructure. And he is doing it with the patience of someone who has already decided he will win.

It is early morning in Nigeria when Sadeeq Ahli picks up the call. He starts talking before the first question is asked.
Sadeeq Ahli
"Most people want to know what I'm building right now. What I want them to understand is why I'm building it this way. The what changes. The why is the whole thing."
Let's start with PitchLink. On the surface, it sounds like a pitch-booking app. Convince me it's more than that.
Sadeeq Ahli
"I understand why it sounds that way. But what we're actually doing is solving a structural problem that happens to express itself as a booking problem. In Nigeria — and across most of Africa — the football ecosystem is completely fragmented. There's no connective tissue. Players can't find pitches. Pitch owners have no digital visibility. Communities have no way to organise. What PitchLink is building is the connective tissue. The booking functionality is the entry point. What comes after it — data, community infrastructure, investment visibility, player pathways — that's the real product."
So PitchLink is a Trojan horse?
Sadeeq Ahli
"I'd call it a foundation. A Trojan horse implies deception. There's no deception here. I've been very clear, at least to myself, about what this is. We're building a layer of infrastructure that doesn't exist. When it exists, it will be very hard to replicate, because the value of the platform comes from the network that uses it. You can copy an app. You cannot copy a network that already trusts you."
The Education No School Gave Him
Where did this systems-thinking orientation come from?
Sadeeq Ahli
"I grew up watching things not work. Infrastructure failing. Supply chains collapsing. Markets that should function but don't, because no one has bothered to build the underlying architecture. Most people see that and feel frustration. I started to feel something different — I started to feel opportunity. Not opportunity in a cynical sense. Genuine opportunity: there is a gap here, and someone needs to close it, and that someone could be me."
That's a significant mental shift.
Sadeeq Ahli
"It is. And it takes time to make it. I think the entrepreneurs who struggle most in African markets are the ones who import frameworks from elsewhere — Silicon Valley, Europe — without interrogating whether those frameworks apply here. The cadence is different. The infrastructure deficit is different. The patient capital is different. You have to build your own mental model for what success looks like on this continent, and it almost certainly looks slower, and more fundamental, than what you've been told."
Expanding the Map: Oil, Gas, and the Industrial Frontier
Talk to us about the expansion into industrial sectors. That feels like a significant pivot from sports technology.
Sadeeq Ahli
"It's not a pivot. That's the thing people misunderstand. I'm not pivoting away from sports tech. I'm building alongside it. The mental model I use for all my ventures is the same: where are the structural inefficiencies in a large-scale system, and where can I build or control a key layer in that system's value chain? That question applies equally to football pitch infrastructure and oil and gas supply chains. The verticals are different. The logic is identical."
But oil and gas is a notoriously difficult sector to enter — particularly as a young entrepreneur without a deep legacy network.
Sadeeq Ahli
"Correct. Which is why I'm not trying to enter it tomorrow. I'm positioning now so that when the moment arrives — when the network is right, when the capital is aligned, when the specific gap I've identified becomes accessible — I'm already in the room. The mistake most entrepreneurs make is they try to enter too fast and too visible. You show up before you have leverage, and you're at a disadvantage. I'd rather take three more years and arrive with leverage."
The Philosophy: Small, Steady, Inevitable
The quote "Stay small enough long enough, and you'll be big enough soon enough" has circulated. What's the fuller version of that thought?
Sadeeq Ahli
"The fuller version is that size at the wrong time is a liability, not an asset. I see founders raise large rounds and suddenly they're managing headcount instead of building product. They're optimising for investor optics instead of market reality. They scale before the foundation is ready and then wonder why the building cracks. Staying small — deliberately, strategically — means you are always operating at the level of reality. You can't fake it. You have to solve real problems because you don't have the cushion to avoid them. That discipline compounds. And when you do scale, you scale on solid ground."
But patience has a cost. Opportunities have windows.
Sadeeq Ahli
"Yes. And knowing the difference between a window and a trap is the entire art of this. I'm not patient for its own sake. I'm patient because I've studied the markets I'm entering deeply enough to know that the leverage point hasn't arrived yet. When it does, I will move very fast. The patience is preparation. It is not passivity."
The African Entrepreneur: Opportunity and Obligation
How do you think about your role as part of a new generation of African builders?
Sadeeq Ahli
"I think about it a lot, actually. There's a version of African entrepreneurship that is essentially about performing modernity — raising money from international VCs, building products that look like scaled-down versions of Western platforms, getting covered in the right publications. That can be meaningful. But it's not what I'm doing. What I'm focused on is economic architecture. Who controls the supply chains? Who controls the platforms that African industries run on? Who controls the infrastructure? Because those are the questions that determine who actually benefits when African economies grow."
That sounds like a political project as much as an economic one.
Sadeeq Ahli
"Every significant business is a political project, whether the founder admits it or not. The choice is whether you're conscious of it. I am conscious of it. I want to build companies that create economic value in a way that is structurally grounded in the African context — that generates opportunities here, that builds capacity here, that positions Africans as owners of their own infrastructure rather than users of infrastructure owned elsewhere. That ambition sits behind everything I do."
What Comes Next
Last question. What does success look like in ten years?
Sadeeq Ahli
"A set of companies that didn't need me to be loud. That quietly became critical infrastructure for the markets they serve. That created opportunities for people — employees, partners, communities — at a scale I can't fully picture yet. And a reputation built not on press coverage, but on the kind of work that speaks for itself long after the moment has passed."
"That's the long game. And I'm playing it."
"Building systems. Creating leverage.
Playing the long game."
